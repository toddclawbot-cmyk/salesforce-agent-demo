Act One — The Lesson

Company A. The call that changed everything.

Maya worked customer support at a mid-size SaaS company. She was good at her job — until one call taught her she was doing it completely wrong.

Memory Retrieved Maya's Personal Vault — 14 months ago
Maya was 6 months into the job. A customer — David Chen, IT Director at a hospital network — called in furious. Their entire patient scheduling system had gone down during a critical surgery window. No patients could be checked in. The OR was backed up.
"We've been down for 47 minutes. I need this fixed NOW. Not a ticket. Not an escalation. FIXED."
— David Chen, IT Director (call recording, 2024)
Maya panicked. She did what she'd been trained to do: follow the script. Reboot the system. Clear the cache. Check the status page. Escalate to Tier 2. She followed every step by the book.
The system came back — 23 minutes later. By then, three surgeries had been delayed. David Chen's hospital had to reroute patients. It was a disaster.
After we hung up, I realized: I never asked what the customer needed right now. I followed steps. He needed someone to listen before they started solving. The best outcome wasn't "fixed" — it was "I hear you, here's what I'm doing, stay with me."
— Post-call reflection, Maya's journal

#lesson #customer-empathy #escalation

The David Chen Incident.

When a customer is in crisis, they don't need a script. They need to know you're present. The first 90 seconds of any escalation call should be empathy, not diagnosis. Acknowledge, then solve.

New framework: "Presence before process." 1. Acknowledge the impact on them (not the system) 2. Give them a window into what's happening 3. Solve with them, not for them
